What is Risk Management?

Consider the case below where you buy Apple stock at $140 a share, and the price jumps to $160 a share. However, you have good intel that the price will tank to $100 a share soon. There is a "risk" that you'll lose money when the price drops. You can offset this risk by buying puts at the $160 strike for $5, so that for every dollar you lose on stock, you gain a dollar from the options.
Say you have an option position that's appreciated $100,000 since you bought it. The position is protected against price changes, but sensitive to increases in volatility (i.e. short lots of vega). If you know volatility will skyrocket, and this would mean big losses, as we've simulated below.
However, all options increase in value when volatility increases, so we can protect against this volatility risk by paying $5 for calls and puts near the current stock price -- this is where the volatility sensitivity is greatest for options.
It's important to consider that options have different sensitivities to volatility and price change at every strike. This means that even if your position is balanced when stock is trading at one price, it will change when the price moves considerably.
